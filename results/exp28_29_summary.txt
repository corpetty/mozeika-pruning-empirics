# Experiment 28 & 29 Results Summary

## Experiment 28: Synthetic Linear Teacher - Controlled Mask Recovery

**Goal:** Validate the statistical mechanics framework in a controlled setting where the true mask is identifiable.

**Setup:**
- Single hidden layer, LINEAR activation (no ReLU)
- W2 frozen random, only W1 masked
- N_in=100, N_h=50, W1_true with p0=0.5 sparsity
- Data: y = W2_true @ (W1_true * H1_true) @ X.T + noise (sigma=0.01)
- M_train=3000 samples, 5 seeds, 25 rho values

**Results:**
- Hamming distances range from ~0.48 to ~0.51 across all rho values
- For rho >= 0.001, H1 collapses to ~0% active neurons in most seeds
- For rho = 1.0 (highest), H1 remains fully active (100%)
- The algorithm does NOT recover the true mask (H1_true has 50% sparsity)
- Most runs converge in 0-1 iterations, suggesting the algorithm is too aggressive

**Interpretation:**
The synthetic linear setting shows that with Fisher-based pruning and the specific update rule used, the network either:
1. Keeps all neurons active (low rho), or
2. Collapses to near-zero active neurons (high rho)

The true mask (50% sparsity) is not recovered, suggesting either:
- The training dynamics don't match the theoretical assumptions
- The Fisher estimation is too noisy
- The update rule needs adjustment

**Goal:** Compare OBD (curvature-weighted) vs magnitude pruning at matched sparsity levels.

**Key Findings:**
1. **Magnitude slightly outperforms OBD** at all sparsity levels (0.1-0.5% absolute improvement)
2. Both methods maintain >97% accuracy even at 95% sparsity
3. OBD shows more variance across sparsity levels
4. Magnitude pruning is more stable and performs better

**Surprising Result:**
Contrary to expectations, OBD (which should theoretically preserve important weights better) performs slightly worse than simple magnitude pruning. This suggests:
- The Fisher diagonal approximation may not capture true importance well
- Magnitude may be a better proxy for importance in this setting
- The iterative fine-tuning helps both methods equally
