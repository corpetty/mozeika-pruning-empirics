Temperature: MAP (T_h=0) is optimal. At T_h=3×10⁻⁵, recovery degrades; at T_h≥10⁻⁴ it breaks. Thermal noise O(T_h) swamps energy signal O(σ²/N)≈10⁻⁵. The Rényi window, if it exists, is not accessible with practical algorithms.

rho_c formula: Works at small η. Off by 100–20,000× across general parameter regimes (ignores N, M/N, σ dependence entirely). Empirical fit R²=0.64 — not reliable enough for practical use.

3. Multi-Layer Extension ✗ NO TRANSITION

Architecture: [10 → 5 (tanh) → 1]. Three regimes: over/critical/underdetermined.

Compare to perceptron: sharp drop never appears. Hamming stays ~0.45–0.51.
Layer Collapse: Active Fraction by Layer
Last layer (layer 2) collapses to 0% active even at ρ=0.
No phase transition in MLPs. The non-convex loss landscape has many local minima. Glauber coordinate descent gets trapped in masks that are locally energy-optimal but globally wrong.

Mean-field interpretation: The perceptron has a dense bipartite interaction graph (every weight → every output), where mean-field theory is exact. Hidden layers create a sparse, layered graph — exactly the regime where mean-field breaks down in spin-glass physics. The transition smears out for the same reason glass transitions smear in finite-dimensional models vs. the Sherrington-Kirkpatrick mean-field model.

4. CNN / LeNet-300-100 on MNIST ✗ NO ADVANTAGE

784→300→100→10, ~266K params. Dense baseline: 97.57% accuracy.
Accuracy After Fine-Tuning: Mozeika vs Magnitude Pruning
Mozeika uses energy-score importance (T→0 fast-learning limit). Magnitude uses |w|.
Magnitude pruning wins at every sparsity level. It distributes sparsity non-uniformly across layers (82% in layer 1, 35% in layer 3), concentrating pruning where it hurts least. Mozeika applies uniform pressure per layer — a structural disadvantage.

The Mozeika energy score in the T→0 limit reduces to a sensitivity metric related to SNIP (Lee et al. 2019), which is already known to be competitive with but not superior to magnitude pruning on standard benchmarks.

5. Definitive Baseline Comparison — Matched Sparsity ⚠ MIXED

64→32 (ReLU)→1, M=512, σ=0.05, 8 seeds. Iterative ρ adjustment ±2% sparsity control.
Test MSE vs Sparsity: All Methods
Lower is better. Error bars = ±1 std (8 seeds).
Mozeika vs L1 vs Mag+Retrain (High Sparsity Focus)
The interesting regime: ≥75% sparsity where methods diverge sharply.
High-sparsity stability is real (≥85%): Mozeika MSE=0.33–0.35 vs Mag+Retrain MSE=0.73–0.81 — a 2.2–2.3× advantage. The double-well energy penalty produces qualitatively different masks that degrade gracefully at extreme sparsity.

However, L1 regularization is competitive throughout. At 65–75% sparsity, L1 beats Mozeika (0.22 vs 0.26–0.32). At 85–90%, L1 also degrades but less dramatically than magnitude pruning. Mozeika only strictly wins vs all baselines at ≥85% sparsity — and L1 achieves similar stability without the coordinate search overhead.

Bottom line: The ρ energy penalty encodes something real about high-sparsity structure. The double-well potential V(h) = αh²(h-1)² + ρ/2·h may have standalone value as a differentiable mask regularizer, independent of the statistical mechanics framing.

Summary

The phase transition is a mean-field result: exact for dense bipartite graphs (linear perceptrons), breaks down in sparse layered graphs (MLPs, CNNs). This is the same physics as the Sherrington-Kirkpatrick → Edwards-Anderson transition in spin glasses. The natural extension is belief propagation on the factor graph (Mézard & Montanari, 2009), not Glauber mean-field. The double-well energy penalty is separately interesting as a practical pruning regularizer in the extreme-sparsity regime.
