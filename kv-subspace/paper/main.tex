\begin{abstract}
We study compression of the key-value (KV) cache in transformer language models
via projection into a learned PCA subspace followed by random rotation and
uniform quantization (SubRotQ). Through 30 experiments across five architectures
(Qwen3-1.7B/14B/32B, Mistral-7B, Phi-4, Llama-3.1-8B), we establish that
\textbf{truncation error from dimension reduction categorically dominates
quantization noise}: on WikiText-2, $k{=}64$, 16-bit (pure truncation) produces
$6.25{\times}$ perplexity degradation while $k{=}128$, 4-bit (no truncation,
aggressive quantization) produces only $0.98{\times}$. This motivates a compression
target of $k{=}128$ (full rank, quantization only), yielding \textbf{$4.00{\times}$
compression at $<2\%$ perplexity cost} on Qwen3-14B. We further find that
\textbf{V compression is not viable at any $k < d_\text{head}$ across all tested
architectures}---including Llama-3.1 which lacks QK-norm---indicating that V
vectors are intrinsically high-dimensional, not an artifact of any specific
normalization scheme. K compression generalizes across architectures and context
lengths ($4{\times}$ compression, ${<}2\%$ PPL on Qwen3/Mistral/Llama; stable to 40K
tokens; near-lossless on downstream reasoning). We recommend \textbf{K-only
compression at $k{=}128$/4-bit} as a production-ready configuration.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Inference with large language models is increasingly memory-bound at long context
lengths. For a 14-billion-parameter model like Qwen3-14B at 32K tokens, the KV
cache alone requires approximately 10\,GB of GPU memory---comparable to the model
weights themselves. This limits the practical context window on consumer hardware
and increases serving costs proportionally with sequence length.

KV cache compression aims to reduce this memory footprint at inference time
without modifying model weights. The central challenge is that KV vectors must be
reconstructed with sufficient fidelity to preserve attention score distributions,
which determines the model's output quality.

Our starting point is the \textbf{Universal Weight Subspace Hypothesis (UWSH)}
\cite{kaushik2025uwsh}, which shows that deep networks trained on diverse tasks
converge to shared low-dimensional parametric subspaces. We ask whether this
structure extends to KV \emph{activation} vectors at inference time: if KV vectors
occupy a stable, low-dimensional subspace per (layer, head), offline PCA
calibration should suffice for compression. Our experiments confirm this---K
vectors achieve cross-domain overlap of $0.70$ and cross-layer overlap of $0.56$,
supporting the viability of offline subspace compression.

We combine subspace projection with \textbf{random rotation quantization} (SubRotQ):
a random orthogonal matrix $R$ is applied before uniform scalar quantization to
spread variance across dimensions and reduce quantization error. This is distinct
from PolarQuant \cite{han2025polarquant}, which uses recursive polar coordinate
transformation with $k$-means codebooks.

Our primary contribution is an empirical characterization of this method across
scales and architectures, producing three actionable findings:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=1.5em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Truncation error is the binding constraint.} The critical design
    variable is $k$ (subspace dimension), not bit depth. At $k{=}128$ (full rank),
    4-bit quantization achieves $<2\%$ WikiText-2 PPL degradation. Practitioners
    should maximize $k$ before minimizing bits; reducing bit depth compensates far
    less than restoring dimensions.
  \item \textbf{V compression is universally intractable.} Key vectors compress
    well across all tested architectures. Value vectors resist subspace compression
    at any $k < d_\text{head}$, regardless of whether the architecture uses QK-norm.
    This is an intrinsic property of V variance structure.
  \item \textbf{K-only compression at $k{=}128$/4-bit is the production
    configuration.} Full-rank 4-bit quantization of K achieves $4.00{\times}$
    compression with $<2\%$ WikiText-2 PPL degradation, generalizes across Qwen3,
    Mistral, and Llama, remains stable through 40K-token contexts, and produces
    near-lossless downstream task accuracy.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\paragraph{KV cache compression.}
Prior work falls broadly into four categories. \emph{Token eviction}
\cite{zhang2023h2o, xiao2024streamingllm} discards KV entries for less important
tokens. \emph{Quantization} \cite{hooper2024kvquant, liu2024kivi, kang2024gear}
reduces bit precision of stored vectors. \emph{Dimensionality reduction} projects
KV vectors to lower-rank representations: KVTC \cite{staniszewski2025kvtc}
combines PCA with dynamic-programming bit allocation and entropy coding for
$20{\times}$ compression; Palu \cite{chang2025palu} decomposes KV projection matrices
via SVD; MatryoshkaKV \cite{park2025matryoshka} trains orthogonal projections via
knowledge distillation; Eigen Attention \cite{yang2024eigen} performs attention
directly in PCA-reduced space; SVDq \cite{wu2025svdq} uses SVD with
importance-aware mixed precision; SQuat \cite{li2025squat} combines subspace
projection with orthogonal rotation quantization. \emph{Architectural
modifications} at training time include GQA \cite{ainslie2023gqa} and DeepSeek
MLA \cite{deepseek2024}.

\paragraph{SubRotQ vs.\ related rotation methods.}
Our method applies a random orthogonal matrix $R \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$
before uniform scalar quantization. This is distinct from two methods named
``PolarQuant'': Han \etal~\cite{han2025polarquant} use recursive polar coordinate
conversion with $k$-means codebooks; Wu \etal~\cite{wu2025polarquant2} exploit
2D RoPE-rotated dimension pairs. In Experiment~22, SubRotQ outperforms Han \etal\
at 4-bit across all $k$ values; at 8-bit the methods are equivalent.

\paragraph{Universal Weight Subspace Hypothesis (UWSH).}
Kaushik \etal~\cite{kaushik2025uwsh} demonstrate that deep networks converge to
shared low-dimensional parameter subspaces. We extend this inquiry to KV
\emph{activation} vectors at inference time. K vectors show cross-domain overlap
$0.70$ and cross-layer overlap $0.56$, confirming offline calibration viability.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Notation}
Let $L$ be the number of layers, $H$ the number of KV heads per layer, and $d$
the head dimension. For each token $t$ at layer $l$, head $h$, the model computes
key vector $\mathbf{k}_{l,h,t} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and value vector
$\mathbf{v}_{l,h,t} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\subsection{Calibration}
Given a calibration corpus $\mathcal{C}$ ($\sim$2K tokens of WikiText-2 train
split), we collect all key vectors via forward hooks and compute for each $(l, h)$:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\mu}_{l,h} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{C}|}\sum_t \mathbf{k}_{l,h,t},
  \qquad
  \mathbf{U}_{l,h} \in \mathbb{R}^{d\times d}
  \text{ from SVD of the centered key matrix.}
\end{equation}

Calibration cost: one forward pass on $\sim$2K tokens. Basis storage:
$L \times H \times d^2 \times 4\,\text{bytes} \approx 45\,\text{MB}$ for
Qwen3-14B ($d=128$).

\subsection{SubRotQ: Compression and Reconstruction}

\begin{algorithm}[t]
  \caption{SubRotQ --- Compress / Reconstruct}
  \label{alg:compress}
  \begin{algorithmic}[1]
    \Require key $\mathbf{k} \in \mathbb{R}^d$, basis
      $\mathbf{U}_{l,h}$, mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{l,h}$,
      rotation $R\in\mathbb{R}^{k\times k}$, integers $k$, $n_\text{bits}$
    \State $\tilde{\mathbf{k}} \gets \mathbf{k} - \boldsymbol{\mu}_{l,h}$
      \Comment{Center}
    \State $\mathbf{z} \gets \mathbf{U}_{l,h}[:, {:}k]^\top \tilde{\mathbf{k}}$
      \Comment{Project to top-$k$ subspace}
    \State $\mathbf{z}' \gets R\,\mathbf{z}$
      \Comment{Random rotation (SubRotQ)}
    \State $q \gets \mathrm{Quantize}(\mathbf{z}', n_\text{bits})$
      \Comment{Uniform scalar quantization}
    \State \textbf{store} $(q, \text{scale}, \text{offset})$
      \Comment{$k \cdot n_\text{bits}$ bits vs $d \cdot 16$ bits}
    \Statex
    \State $\mathbf{z}' \gets \mathrm{Dequantize}(q)$
    \State $\mathbf{z} \gets R^\top \mathbf{z}'$
    \State $\hat{\mathbf{k}} \gets \mathbf{U}_{l,h}[:, {:}k]\,\mathbf{z}
           + \boldsymbol{\mu}_{l,h}$
      \Comment{Reconstruct in $\mathbb{R}^d$}
  \end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The compression ratio is:
\begin{equation}
  \text{CR} = \frac{d \cdot 16}{k \cdot n_\text{bits}}.
  \label{eq:cr}
\end{equation}
For $k{=}128$, $n{=}4$, $d{=}128$: $\text{CR} = 2048/512 = 4.00{\times}$.

\subsection{Recommended Deployment}
\label{sec:deployment}

Based on our experiments, the recommended configuration is:
\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{K}: SubRotQ at $k{=}128$, 4-bit --- $4.00{\times}$ compression, ${<}2\%$ PPL cost
  \item \textbf{V}: Full-dimensional 4-bit quantization ($k{=}d{=}128$) --- $4.00{\times}$
  \item \textbf{Combined KV}: $4.00{\times}$ compression
\end{itemize}

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\paragraph{Protocol.}
All PPL experiments use WikiText-2 (train split for calibration, test split for
evaluation; no overlap). Relative PPL $= \text{PPL}_\text{compressed} /
\text{PPL}_\text{baseline}$. Viability threshold: $\relppl < 1.20$. All
experiments run on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 (24\,GB) via SLURM.

%--------------------------------------
\subsection{Subspace Geometry: UWSH Validation (Exp.~1)}
\label{sec:uwsh}

We verify that K activation vectors exhibit the stable low-dimensional structure
required for offline compression by computing principal angles between PCA
subspaces (90\% variance threshold) for three comparison types on Qwen3-14B-AWQ:
\emph{cross-layer}, \emph{cross-head}, and \emph{cross-domain} (fiction vs.\ factual).

\textbf{Results.} K cross-domain overlap $= 0.703$; cross-layer $= 0.565$;
cross-head $= 0.456$.

\textbf{Implications.} Cross-domain overlap $0.70$ confirms a single offline
calibration corpus generalizes across deployment domains. Cross-head overlap
$0.46$ confirms per-head bases are necessary. Higher V cross-layer overlap
($0.74$) indicates more globally uniform V geometry---yet V resists truncation
(Section~\ref{sec:v_failure}).

\subsection{Latency and Throughput (Exps.~26, 29)}
\label{sec:latency}

The current hook-based Python implementation incurs \textbf{1.6$\times$ decode
slowdown} due to GPU$\leftrightarrow$CPU transfers and Python dispatch overhead.
A Torch GPU-native path (no CPU copy) incurs 2.1$\times$. Production deployment
requires a fused CUDA kernel, which we leave as future work.

Memory savings at $k{=}128$/4-bit for Qwen3-14B at 32K context:
uncompressed K cache 5.24\,GB $\rightarrow$ compressed 1.31\,GB (4.00$\times$
reduction, 3.93\,GB saved). Savings scale linearly with batch size and context
length.

\section{Analysis: Why Does Truncation Dominate?}
\label{sec:analysis}

The PCA decomposition partitions variance into orthogonal components ordered by
magnitude. The tail components represent systematic directions that are
\emph{always} absent under truncation---their absence is a constant, correlated
error.

\paragraph{Attention score sensitivity.}
Attention score $\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}$ propagates compression error directly.
For rare but important tokens (names, numbers, technical terms), attention must be
precise; tail K components may align with the specific query directions selecting
such tokens.

\paragraph{Layer-wise accumulation.}
Quantization noise averages out across 40 layers (random, uncorrelated).
Truncation error accumulates---the same dimensions are absent at every layer.
This explains the disproportionate $k{=}64$ degradation in end-to-end evaluation
vs.\ single-layer distortion metrics.

\paragraph{V intrinsic dimensionality.}
The $\sim$30\% of V variance in tail dimensions is qualitatively different from K
tail variance. Online basis updating (Exp.~19) and larger subspaces (Exp.~20)
both fail to recover V quality, suggesting V signal is structurally spread across
all dimensions. This is consistent with V vectors representing token content
(arbitrary, high-dimensional), while K vectors represent relational structure
(more constrained).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What this means for practitioners.}
When tuning compression, maximize $k$ first. $k{=}128$ (full rank) with 4-bit
quantization achieves $<2\%$ WikiText-2 PPL at $4.00{\times}$ compression---the
recommended production configuration for models $\geq 7$B. Do not truncate below
full rank unless memory constraints are severe and your specific architecture
tolerates it (test with WikiText-2 PPL as a proxy).

\paragraph{Large models compress better.}
Within the Qwen3 family, compression tolerance increases monotonically with scale,
consistent with larger models developing lower-rank functional KV subspaces due to
over-parameterization.

\paragraph{Comparison to prior art.}
KVTC \cite{staniszewski2025kvtc} achieves $20{\times}$ compression via PCA +
dynamic-programming bit allocation + entropy coding. Our method achieves $4{\times}$
with simpler engineering (offline PCA + random rotation, no DP or entropy coder).
Palu \cite{chang2025palu} achieves $11.4{\times}$ by decomposing KV projection weight
matrices, requiring weight modification. MatryoshkaKV \cite{park2025matryoshka}
requires knowledge distillation at training time. Our contribution is an
empirically rigorous characterization of the truncation-vs-quantization tradeoff
at breadth (5 architectures, 30 experiments, standard benchmarks) exceeding prior
work, combined with a simple post-hoc method requiring no retraining.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Latency overhead.} The hook-based implementation incurs $1.6{\times}$
decode slowdown. Production deployment requires a fused CUDA kernel.

\paragraph{Small-model limitation.} Models below $\sim$5B parameters (Qwen3-1.7B
in our tests) require $k{=}d$ to stay within the 20\% budget---no subspace benefit.
The method is most impactful for $\geq$7B models.

\paragraph{Basis storage.} Per-(layer, head) PCA bases require $\sim$45\,MB for
Qwen3-14B---modest but non-zero.

\subsection{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\begin{itemize}[itemsep=2pt]
  \item \textbf{Fused CUDA kernel.} Would reduce decode overhead from $1.6{\times}$ to
    an estimated $1.1$--$1.2{\times}$, making the method throughput-positive.
  \item \textbf{100K+ context scaling.} V basis drift (overlap $0.702$ at 40K)
    likely worsens at 100K+. Periodic basis refresh may be necessary.
  \item \textbf{V-norm at training time.} If QK-norm reduces K intrinsic
    dimensionality, an analogous V-norm might make V compression viable.
  \item \textbf{Non-standard KV formats.} DeepSeek MLA \cite{deepseek2024} and
    other latent KV schemes may interact with subspace quantization in unexplored ways.
\end{itemize}

%------------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have systematically characterized SubRotQ---subspace rotation
quantization for KV cache compression---across 30 experiments on six model
variants. The central finding---that truncation error categorically dominates
quantization noise---resolves a longstanding ambiguity: maximize subspace
dimension $k$ before minimizing bit depth. At $k{=}128$/4-bit (full rank,
quantization only), Qwen3-14B achieves \textbf{$4.00{\times}$ KV cache compression
with $<2\%$ WikiText-2 perplexity cost}, stable across 40K context lengths,
calibrated on 2K tokens of generic text, and near-lossless on downstream reasoning
tasks. The finding generalizes to Mistral-7B and Llama-3.1-8B without
architecture-specific tuning.

The secondary finding---that V compression is architecture-independently
intractable below full rank---closes the most natural extension of the method and
redirects future work toward fundamental improvements in V vector compressibility
via learned projections or weight fusion.

We recommend \textbf{K-only SubRotQ at $k{=}128$/4-bit} as a production-ready
configuration for models $\geq$7B parameters.
